"In Plato's Cave" - Response

Photography is one of my absolute dearest forms of expression. One of the ways I use photography is as a way to connect with other people who share similar experiences. When I take photos, I don’t want to just scratch the surface—I want to go deeper, doing my absolute best to capture its essence, and in a way that I’m certain my people can understand. The prospect of someone looking at one of my images and seeing a part of themselves in it, even if they don’t know my exact story, is one of my greatest pleasures. Photography allows me to communicate sentiments that can’t adequately be explained through words alone.


Creativity also plays a huge role in how I make photos. Photography is the most accessible it’s ever been; more people than ever around the world have a camera right in their pocket. So, I feel extra motivated to push myself creatively. I like to experiment with angles, exposure, flash, subject matter, and other tools; I want my images to stand out. I enjoy trying new ideas and taking risks, even if the execution isn’t always the best.


When I swapped phones with my brother earlier this year, I got a creative awakening. I took thousands of photos on my iPhone 15 Pro just this year alone (note: do NOT upgrade your iPhone every year, they are built off slave minerals to say the least). I got a sudden urge to take frequent walks throughout the city, whether it was down 5th Avenue in Midtown, Riverside Drive in Harlem, or in Washington Square Park. My "Fortwo" image, along with the very background of this website, was actually shot by me at Riverside back in November. I loved testing my photography skills in the many environments I came across.


As I continue working on projects throughout the semester, each project is a chance for me to improve and build on what I previously learned. I hope to reach a point where my technical skills are unmistakable in my work. Showing a high level of skill and effort is also important to me because it reflects growth and dedication. I want to get to a point where I’m confident in my abilities and I can use photography to the highest degree of expertise.


I also like to incorporate satire into my work to some extent. I like to keep it real and make people laugh; using irony in the things that I make allows me to comment on personal or social experiences without being too serious or direct. To be honest, I think that things don’t always have to be that deep. Being a tad unserious makes some of the media I make more layered. Depending on what I make, people have the option to engage with my work on different levels, depending on how closely they look.


All in all, I’m drawn to themes of shared experience, identity, and emotion. Photography has the power to freeze moments that reflect larger ideas about society. This is all too important, especially in a time when people are “chronically online”, constantly scrolling and interacting with others on social media instead of interacting with the world around them. My photography skills are something I see as constantly evolving for me. Each photo I take helps me learn more about my style and what I want to communicate. I truly want to create work that feels meaningful. Not only to me, but also to the people who find my work and hopefully learn to love it. I want to continue my growth as a creator of media and to continue using photography as a way to reflect both my personal experiences and the world around me.
